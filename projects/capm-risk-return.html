01 — The Model
Capital Asset Pricing Model
What is CAPM?

CAPM is a foundational model in finance that links an asset's expected return to its systematic risk (beta) relative to a market index.

The model decomposes risk into systematic (market-wide, non-diversifiable) and idiosyncratic (firm-specific, diversifiable). CAPM rewards only systematic risk.

Beta measures sensitivity to market movements: β=1 moves with the market, β>1 amplifies it, β<1 dampens it.

Formula & Parameters
CAPM Formula E(rA) = rf + βA × (E(rm) − rf)

βA — Computed as Cov(rA, rm) / Var(rm) over the selected period

rf — 10-year U.S. Treasury yield (~4.2% p.a. → converted to monthly)

E(rm) — Historical arithmetic mean return of S&P 500 over the selected window

03 — Results
Beta vs. CAPM Expected Return
Each point = one stock · Dashed line = Security Market Line (SML)
Reading the Security Market Line
  • The dashed SML represents the CAPM-predicted return for every level of beta. Any stock lying exactly on the line has zero alpha — it earns exactly what its systematic risk warrants.
  • Above the SML — the stock has generated more return than CAPM predicts for its beta. This positive alpha suggests outperformance that CAPM alone cannot explain.
  • Below the SML — the stock underperformed relative to its risk level. Negative alpha means investors were not adequately compensated for the systematic risk taken.
  • The slope of the SML equals the market risk premium: E(rm) − rf. A steeper SML means higher reward per unit of beta in the selected period.
Understanding Beta & CAPM Limitations
  • β < 1 — defensive stock, less volatile than the market. Utilities and Consumer Staples typically cluster here.
  • β = 1 — moves in lockstep with the S&P 500. The market itself has β = 1 by definition.
  • β > 1 — aggressive stock, amplifies market moves. High-growth tech tends to sit here.
  • CAPM assumes historical beta persists — it does not. It also ignores liquidity, size, value and momentum factors, which is why non-zero alphas are common and expected in practice.

Jensen's Alpha (α)
  • Alpha is the y-intercept of the OLS regression line fitted to excess returns. It represents the return the stock generates when the market return equals zero — i.e. pure outperformance beyond what beta predicts.
  • Positive α — the stock consistently outperformed what CAPM expected given its risk. The stock generated excess returns not explained by its market exposure.
  • Negative α — the stock underdelivered relative to its risk level. CAPM over-predicted performance, suggesting company-specific headwinds.
  • A non-zero alpha means CAPM is insufficient to fully explain this stock's returns — other factors (value, momentum, quality) are likely at play.
Treynor Ratio
  • Treynor = (mean excess return × 12) / β. It measures how much annualised excess return an asset delivers per unit of systematic risk (beta).
  • Unlike Sharpe, Treynor ignores idiosyncratic (diversifiable) risk. It is most meaningful for assets held as part of a diversified portfolio, where unsystematic risk is already eliminated.
  • Higher Treynor — more return per unit of market risk. Better risk-adjusted performance relative to systematic exposure.
  • Comparing Treynor across stocks tells you which generates the best return for the same amount of market sensitivity — useful for portfolio construction.
Sharpe Ratio
  • Sharpe = (mean excess return / σ of excess returns) × √12. It measures return per unit of total risk — both systematic and idiosyncratic.
  • Unlike Treynor, Sharpe penalises all volatility, making it ideal for evaluating standalone positions or concentrated portfolios where diversification is limited.
  • Higher Sharpe — more return per unit of overall price volatility. The investment compensates investors well for the turbulence endured.
  • A Sharpe above 1.0 is generally considered good; above 2.0 is excellent. Negative Sharpe means the asset underperformed the risk-free rate on a risk-adjusted basis.

Rolling Beta (β)
  • Rather than computing one beta over the entire period, rolling beta calculates it repeatedly over a sliding window (e.g. the last 12 months), then moves one month forward and repeats.
  • This reveals how a stock's sensitivity to market movements evolves over time — something a static beta completely hides.
  • A rising rolling beta means the stock is becoming more correlated with the market — possibly due to macro regime changes, increased leverage, or sector rotation.
  • A falling rolling beta may signal the stock is decoupling from the market — driven by company-specific catalysts, defensive repositioning, or idiosyncratic events.
Rolling Alpha (α)
  • Rolling alpha tracks the OLS intercept over each window — the period-specific excess return above what beta-adjusted CAPM would predict.
  • Sustained positive rolling alpha over multiple windows suggests persistent outperformance that CAPM cannot explain — a strong signal of value creation beyond market exposure.
  • Periods of sharp negative alpha often coincide with company-specific crises, earnings misses, or sector-wide stress that disproportionately hurt this stock versus the market.
  • The dashed reference line at α = 0 represents the CAPM theoretical ideal. Most stocks oscillate around it over time.
Rolling R²
  • R² measures the proportion of the stock's return variance explained by market movements in each window. Range: 0 (no correlation) to 1 (perfectly explained by market).
  • High R² — the stock is behaving like the market. Systematic risk dominates; idiosyncratic factors are minimal in this period.
  • Low R² — company-specific factors are driving returns more than the market is. CAPM explains little of this stock's behaviour in this window.
  • A declining R² often precedes periods of high alpha (positive or negative) — as CAPM loses explanatory power, stock-specific events take over.
04 — Methodology
Data & Computation
  • Monthly adjusted closes pre-processed from a 500MB HDF5 file into a lean ~4MB JSON, hosted on GitHub and fetched once on page load — no API calls needed
  • Returns computed as month-over-month percentage change in adjusted closes
  • Beta via OLS on excess returns: β = Cov(ri − rf, rm − rf) / Var(rm − rf), matching the statsmodels OLS approach in the original Python app
  • Jensen's Alpha = OLS y-intercept (α) × 12 — annualised excess return when the market return equals zero
  • Sharpe = (mean excess return / σ of excess returns) × √12 · Treynor = (mean excess return × 12) / β
  • Rolling CAPM uses a sliding window of configurable size (6–36 months) recomputing β, α and R² at each step
  • Outliers removed with the IQR method (×1.5 fence) before OLS regression — mirrors the original Python implementation
Assumptions & Limitations
  • Risk-free rate is manually set; the tool uses a fixed annual rate converted to the matching interval
  • CAPM is a single-factor model — idiosyncratic risk, liquidity, and macro factors are not captured
  • Historical beta is backward-looking; it may not reflect current or future market sensitivity
  • Data quality depends on Yahoo Finance's API response; missing data is handled by forward-filling the last available close
  • This tool is for educational purposes — not investment advice
