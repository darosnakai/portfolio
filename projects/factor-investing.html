Equity Factor
Investing Strategy

A long-short factor investing strategy built on three academically-grounded risk premia — Value, Momentum, and Quality — constructed from scratch on the S&P 500 universe using MSCI-aligned scoring methodology, sector-neutral z-scores, and beta-neutral (BAB) portfolio construction. Each factor is validated independently and combined into a multi-factor portfolio benchmarked against the S&P 500. Portfolios are rebalanced at the end of each month.

Methodology
Why these factors persist

Factor premia are explained by a combination of risk-based and behavioural arguments. Risk-based theories hold that higher expected returns compensate investors for bearing systematic risks that cannot be diversified away. Behavioural theories argue that cognitive biases — overreaction, anchoring, herding — create persistent mispricings that rational investors can exploit.

  • Value: Cheap stocks carry higher fundamental risk (distress, low growth expectations). Investors also systematically overprice glamour stocks and underprice value stocks due to extrapolation bias.
  • Momentum: Underreaction to news causes prices to drift in the direction of recent returns. Trend-following and disposition effects reinforce the anomaly.
  • Quality: Investors underweight the persistence of high-quality earnings. High profitability is not fully reflected in prices due to limited attention and accounting complexity.
Score construction
  • Value Score = avg[z(−P/B), z(−P/E), z(DY)] — following MSCI Global Factor Index methodology
  • Momentum Score = avg[z(12M ret), z(6M ret), z(3M ret), z(dist MA200), z(dist VWAP)]
  • Quality Score = avg[z(ROE), z(ROA), z(Gross Profit/Assets), z(FCF/Assets), z(CF/NI), z(−Accruals)]
  • All z-scores are sector-neutral (computed within GICS sector × date groups) to avoid loading on sector composition
  • All metrics winsorised at 1st/99th percentile before scoring to remove outlier contamination
General Score Formula Scorei,t = (1/N) Σ zsector(winsorise(xi,t))
Data sources
  • Price data: EODHD API — adjusted daily close, volume, high/low for all S&P 500 constituents. Stored in HDF5 format keyed by ticker.
  • Fundamentals: EODHD quarterly statements — Income Statement, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow. Filing dates used to shift signals to the next trading day after announcement.
  • Dividend Yield: EODHD dividend history — annualised DPS / price, recomputed daily.
  • S&P 500 benchmark: daily index returns 1976–2026, compounded to monthly for comparison.
Universe construction
  • All tickers that were ever constituents of the S&P 500 — survivorship bias explicitly addressed by including historical members.
  • Once a stock exits the index, it is removed from the investable universe from that date forward.
  • Minimum price filter: $5. Minimum average dollar volume: $1M/day (252-day rolling average).
  • Minimum beta history: 252 trading days required before a stock can enter any portfolio.
  • Common backtest window: 2005–2026 — the overlap of all three factor date ranges.
Long-short construction
  • At each month-end rebalance date, stocks are ranked by their factor score within the investable universe.
  • Long leg: top 30% by score (highest quality/cheapest/strongest momentum)
  • Short leg: bottom 30% by score
  • Equal weights within each leg — each stock receives 1/N of the leg's capital.
  • Minimum 150 names per leg required for a rebalance to proceed (clean universe filter).
Beta-neutral scaling (BAB)

Following Frazzini & Pedersen (2014), each leg is scaled by the inverse of its ex-ante beta to achieve beta neutrality with respect to the market:

BAB Return Formula rBAB = (1/βL) · rL − (1/βH) · rH
  • Rolling 252-day beta estimated as Cov(ri, rm) / Var(rm) for each stock at each rebalance date.
  • Beta bounds: [0.05, 3.0] — extreme betas excluded.
  • Monthly leg returns computed first, then BAB scaling applied — no daily compounding of the spread.
Validation checks performed
  • Stock counts per leg monitored each month — portfolios with fewer than 150 names excluded.
  • Return sanity checks: extreme monthly returns (|r| > 50%) investigated and traced to specific stocks/events.
  • Sign consistency: confirmed that long legs have higher factor scores than short legs at each rebalance.
  • Look-ahead bias: fundamentals shifted to next trading day after filing date; momentum signals skip most-recent month (t−1).
  • Duplicate row checks performed on all HDF5 data layers before any portfolio construction.
Known limitations
  • No transaction costs. High-turnover factors (Momentum) are most affected. Real-world implementation would require netting trades across factors.
  • No borrowing costs. Short-selling feasibility and locate costs are not modelled.
  • Equal weights within legs. Size and liquidity biases are not controlled beyond the dollar-volume filter.
  • Dividend Yield data starts 2005. F_DY sub-factor history is limited; the agglomerate Value score falls back to P/B and P/E only for any tickers with missing DY.
Literature
Key References
Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (1992)
The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns. The Journal of Finance, 47(2), 427–465.
The foundational paper establishing that size and book-to-market ratio explain the cross-section of expected returns — the direct academic basis for the Value factor. Introduced the two-factor extension to CAPM and catalysed the entire factor investing literature.
Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (1993)
Common risk factors in the returns on stocks and bonds. Journal of Financial Economics, 33(1), 3–56.
Formalises the three-factor model (market, size, value) that became the standard benchmark for evaluating alpha. The HML (High-Minus-Low) factor is the direct precursor to the Value factor implemented in this project.
Jegadeesh, N., & Titman, S. (1993)
Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers: Implications for Stock Market Efficiency. The Journal of Finance, 48(1), 65–91.
The seminal paper establishing price momentum as a persistent anomaly in the US equity market. Documents that stocks with strong 3–12 month past returns continue to outperform. The core motivation for the Momentum factor in this project.
Frazzini, A., & Pedersen, L. H. (2014)
Betting Against Beta. Journal of Financial Economics, 111(1), 1–23.
Introduces the BAB factor and the leverage-constrained investor framework that explains why low-beta assets earn higher risk-adjusted returns. The BAB scaling methodology used in this project to construct beta-neutral portfolios follows directly from this paper.
Asness, C. S., Frazzini, A., & Pedersen, L. H. (2019)
Quality Minus Junk. Review of Accounting Studies, 24(1), 34–112.
Defines and validates the Quality factor using profitability, growth, safety, and payout metrics. The composite scoring approach and the specific metrics used in this project — ROE, ROA, accruals, FCF — are closely aligned with the QMJ framework from this paper.
MSCI Inc. (2021)
MSCI Global Investable Market Indexes Methodology. MSCI Index Methodology Documentation, February 2021.
The official MSCI documentation for factor index construction — including sector-neutral z-scoring, winsorisation procedures, and composite score aggregation. The Value and Momentum scoring methodology in this project follows the MSCI specification as closely as the available data permits.
Blitz, D., Hanauer, M. X., Vidojevic, M., & van Vliet, P. (2018)
Five Concerns with the Five-Factor Model. Robeco Quantitative Research — Factor Investing: How Many Factors Are There? Or How to Navigate the 'Factor Zoo'.
A practitioner-oriented Robeco research paper examining factor proliferation and the challenge of selecting robust, independent factors from the academic literature. Directly relevant to the decision to focus on three well-established, economically-motivated factors in this project.
